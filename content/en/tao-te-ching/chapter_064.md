# 道德经（Dao De Jing）
## 第64章

That which is stable is easy to hold; that which has not yet shown signs is easy to plan. The fragile is easily broken, the minute easily scattered. Act when it has not yet come into being; govern before disorder arises. A tree that can be grasped in both arms grows from a tiny sprout; a nine‑storey tower rises from a heap of earth; a journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step. Those who act ruin it, those who grasp lose it. Therefore the sage, by non‑action, does not fail; by non‑grasping, does not lose. The people, in their enterprises, often fail when they are on the verge of success. If they are as cautious at the end as at the beginning, they will not be defeated. Thus the sage desires to have no desires, does not prize hard‑to‑get goods; learns not to learn, and corrects the excesses of the multitude. By assisting the naturalness of all things he does not dare to act.

原文：其安易持，其未兆易谋，其脆易泮，其微易散。为之于未有，治之于未乱。合抱之木，生于毫末；九层之台，起于累土；千里之行，始于足下。为者败之，执者失之。是以圣人无为故无败，无执故无失。民之从事，常于几成而败之。慎终如始，则无败事。是以圣人欲不欲，不贵难得之货；学不学，复众人之所过。以辅万物之自然而不敢为。
