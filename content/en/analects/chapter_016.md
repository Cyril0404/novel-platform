# The Analects of Confucius
## Book 16: Ji Shi (The head of the Ji family)

The head of the Ji family was greater than any other noble in the state, but he still assumed the nine squares of the territory, and did not submit to the king. Confucius said, "If he is not able to govern a country with the rules of propriety, how can he assume the nine squares?"

Confucius said of the head of the Ji family, who had eight rows of dancers performing in his courtyard: "If he can bear to do this, what may he not bear to do?"

The three families used the Yong ode in the removal of the sacrifices after the feast. The Master said: "'Assisted are the princes, the son of heaven looks profound and grave'--what application can these words have in the hall of the three families?"

The head of the Ji family was going to sacrifice to Mount Tai. The Master said to Ran Qiu, "Can you not save him from this?" He answered, "I cannot." The Master said, "Alas! Will you say that Mount Tai is not so discerning as Lin Fang?"

The Master said, "A youth is to be regarded with respect. How do we know that his future will not be equal to our present? If he reach the age of forty or fifty, and has not made himself heard of, then indeed he will not be worth being regarded with respect."

The Master said, "The officers may not be without breadth of mind and vigorous endurance. Their burden is heavy and their course is long. Perfect virtue is the burden which they consider it is theirs to sustain; is it not heavy? Only with death does their course stop; is it not long?"

The Master said, "When the principles of one's nature are developed, he becomes wise; when wisdom is developed, he becomes discriminating; when discrimination is developed, he becomes forewarned; forewarned, he becomes capable of ruling."

The Master said, "From the man bringing his bundle of dried flesh for my teaching upwards, I have never refused instruction to any one."

The Master said, "The men of former times wished to be distinguished in the empire, and they were distinguished. The men of these latter times wish to be distinguished in their villages, but they are not distinguished."

The Master said, "The ancients studied for their own improvement; the moderns study for the approval of others."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind, does not do what is not right."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind is not found in wrong positions."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind has no cause for grief."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind is not influenced by poverty."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind is not injured by slander."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind is not troubled by the world."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind is not lost in the world."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind has no enemies."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind is not matched by any one."

The three things of the mind referred to above are: seriousness, love, and畏 reverence. Earnestness guards against offenses; love wins the hearts of the people; and reverence makes for carefulness in one's conduct.

The Master said, "The wise man is not troubled by anything; the virtuous man is not anxious about anything; the courageous man is not afraid of anything."

The Master said, "When you are living in a state, take service with its most worthy officers, and make friends of its most virtuous scholars."

The Master said, "The man who is not in any particular office has nothing to do with plans for the administration of its duties."

The Master said, "When a country is well governed, poverty and a mean condition are things to be ashamed of. When a country is ill governed, riches and honor are things to be ashamed of."

The Master said, "If a prince can himself practice the five virtues, what difficulty will he have in government?"

The Master said, "To govern a country of a thousand chariots, there must be reverent attention to business, and sincerity."

The Master said, "If a ruler is personally correct, his government is effective without the issuing of orders. If his personal conduct is not correct, he may issue orders, but they will not be followed."

The Master said, "The governments of Lu and Wei are brothers."

The Master said of Jing, Duke of Wei, that he understood the regulating of the household.

The Master said, "The affairs of a country may be learned from its ministers."

The Master said, "The three things of the mind are: earnestness, love, and reverence. Earnestness guards against offenses; love wins the hearts of the people; reverence makes for carefulness in conduct."

The Master said, "If a prince can himself practice the five virtues, what difficulty will he have in government?"

The Master said, "To know it is not as good as to love it; to love it is not as good as to delight in it."

The Master said, "Having once made a promise, it should be kept. To fail in keeping it is to make a breach in one's own character."

The Master said, "The commander of the forces of a large state may be carried off, but the will of even a common man cannot be taken from him."

The Master said, "The cautious seldom err."

The Master said, "The superior man wishes to be slow in his speech and earnest in his conduct."

The Master said, "Virtue is not left to stand alone. He who practices it will have neighbors."

The Master said, "The superior man is satisfied and composed; the mean man is always full of distress."

The Master said, "The superior man is easy to serve, but difficult to please. The mean man is difficult to serve, but easy to please."

The Master said, "The superior man thinks of virtue; the mean man thinks of comfort."

### 原文
季氏将伐颛臾。冉有、季路见于孔子曰：“季氏将有事于颛臾。”孔子曰：“求！无乃尔是过与？夫颛臾，昔者先王以为东蒙主，且在邦域之中矣，是社稷之臣也。何以伐为？”冉有曰：“夫子欲之，吾二臣者皆不欲也。”孔子曰：“求！周任有言曰：‘陈力就列，不能者止。’危而不持，颠而不扶，则将焉用彼相矣？且尔言过矣。虎兕出于柙，龟玉毁于椟中，是谁之过与？”冉有曰：“今夫颛臾，固而近于费。今不取，后世必为子孙忧。”孔子曰：“求！君子疾夫舍曰欲之而必为之辞。丘也闻有国有家者，不患寡而患不均，不患贫而患不安。盖均无贫，和无寡，安无倾。夫如是，故远人不服，则修文德以来之。既来之，则安之。今由与求也，相夫子，远人不服而不能来也，邦分崩离析而不能守也；而谋动干戈于邦内。吾恐季孙之忧，不在颛臾，而在萧墙之内也。”
孔子曰：“天下有道，则礼乐征伐自天子出；天下无道，则礼乐征伐自诸侯出。自诸侯出，盖十世希不失矣；自大夫出，五世希不失矣；陪臣执国命，三世希不失矣。天下有道，则政不在大夫。天下有道，则庶人不议。”
孔子曰：“禄之去公室五世矣，政逮于大夫四世矣，故夫三桓之子孙微矣。”
孔子曰：“益者三友，损者三友。友直，友谅，友多闻，益矣。友便辟，友善柔，友便佞，损矣。”
孔子曰：“益者三乐，损者三乐。乐节礼乐，乐道人之善，乐多贤友，益矣。乐骄乐，乐佚游，乐晏乐，损矣。”
孔子曰：“侍于君子有三愆：言未及之而言谓之躁，言及之而不言谓之隐，未见颜色而言谓之瞽。”
孔子曰：“君子有三戒：少之时，血气未定，戒之在色；及其壮也，血气方刚，戒之在斗；及其老也，血气既衰，戒之在得。”
孔子曰：“君子有三畏：畏天命，畏大人，畏圣人之言。小人不知天命而不畏也，狎大人，侮圣人之言。”
孔子曰：“生而知之者，上也；学而知之者，次也；困而学之，又其次也；困而不学，民斯为下矣。”
孔子曰：“君子有九思：视思明，听思聪，色思温，貌思恭，言思忠，事思敬，疑思问，忿思难，见得思义。”
孔子曰：“见善如不及，见不善如探汤。吾见其人矣，吾闻其语矣。隐居以求其志，行义以达其道。吾闻其语矣，未见其人也。”
齐景公有马千驷，死之日，民无德而称焉。伯夷、叔齐饿于首阳之下，民到于今称之。其斯之谓与？
陈亢问于伯鱼曰：“子亦有异闻乎？”对曰：“未也。尝独立，鲤趋而过庭。曰：‘学《诗》乎？’对曰：‘未也。’‘不学《诗》，无以言。’鲤退而学《诗》。他日，又独立，鲤趋而过庭。曰：‘学礼乎？’对曰：‘未也。’‘不学礼，无以立。’鲤退而学礼。闻斯二者。”陈亢退而喜曰：“问一得三，闻《诗》，闻礼，又闻君子之远其子也。”
邦君之妻，君称之曰夫人，夫人自称曰小童；邦人称之曰君夫人，称诸异邦曰寡小君；异邦人称之亦曰君夫人。
