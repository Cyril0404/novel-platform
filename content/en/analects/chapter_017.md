# The Analects of Confucius
## Book 17: Yang Huo (Yang Huo)

Yang Huo wished to see Confucius, but Confucius would not go to see him. He sent a present of a pig's tongue to Confucius, who chose to go to his house at a time when he was not there, to return the visit. Huo happened to meet him on the way, and said, "Come, I wish to speak with you." He answered, "I am not able to do so." Huo said, "Are you too busy to cultivate your field, and so pass your time?" Confucius was silent. Huo then said, "Are you too old to be idle?" Upon this Confucius again gave him an answer. He said, "Yes, I am old." Huo said, "The prince is indolent, and the ministers are luxurious, and the people are disaffected. The State is going to ruin. I wish to correct it." Confucius said, "If the country were well governed, I would not be here to mend it."

The Master said, "Following without attaining to the constant virtue, saying without attaining to the weight of one's words, cultivating the inferior arts: from which of these does the man of complete virtue shrink?"

The Master said, "The five things which the man of complete virtue pracrices are: gravity, generosity of soul, sincerity, straightforwardness, and benignity. If he is grave, he does not suffer disrespect. If he is generous, he wins all hearts. If he is sincere, men put trust in him. If he is straightforward, he is without insolence. If he is benign, he is able tocommand the service of others."

The Master said, "A country of a thousand chariots cannot be governed without careful attention to business and sincerity."

The Master said, "If a prince can himself practice the five virtues, what difficulty will he have in government?"

The Master said, "Let him who exercises government by the rules of propriety be like the north polar star, which keeps its place, and all the stars turn towards it."

The Master said, "If a man can for one day subdue himself and return to propriety, all under heaven will ascribe perfect virtue to him."

The Master said, "To subdue one's self and return to propriety, is perfect virtue."

The Master said, "The superior man is humble in his virtue, respectful in his service to others, and moderate in his desires."

The Master said, "The man who is not trusted in a state, will never get the confidence of the people."

The Master said, "The government of a country may be learned from its ministers."

The Master said, "The affairs of a country may be learned from its ministers."

The Master said, "When a country is well governed, poverty and a mean condition are things to be ashamed of. When a country is ill governed, riches and honor are things to be ashamed of."

The Master said, "The officer may not be without breadth of mind and vigorous endurance. His burden is heavy and his course is long."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind is not found in wrong positions."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind does not do what is not right."

The Master said, "The man who cultivates the three things of the mind is not troubled by the world."

The three things of the mind are: earnestness, love, and reverence.

The Master said, "If a man can for one day subdue himself and return to propriety, all under heaven will ascribe perfect virtue to him."

The Master said, "To subdue one's self and return to propriety, is perfect virtue."

The Master said, "The superior man wishes to be slow in his speech and earnest in his conduct."

The Master said, "The cautious seldom err."

The Master said, "The commander of the forces of a large state may be carried off, but the will of even a common man cannot be taken from him."

The Master said, "Having once made a promise, it should be kept. To fail in keeping it is to make a breach in one's own character."

The Master said, "To know it is not as good as to love it; to love it is not as good as to delight in it."

The Master said, "Having read much and remembering well, and being a man of complete virtue, what difficulty would there be in such a one?"

The Master said, "The wise are free from perplexities; the virtuous from anxiety; the bold from fear."

The Master said, "When I was young, my manners were simple; when I was learning, I was poor; now that I am old, I am in danger of not being thought wise."

The Master said, "The man who in the morning hears the right way, and in the evening dies, may be pronounced to have lived aright."

The Master said, "I have not yet seen one who really loved virtue, and one who hated vice."

The Master said, "The superior man is distressed by his want of ability; he is not distressed by men's not knowing him."

The Master said, "The superior man is humble in his virtue, respectful in his service to others, and moderate in his desires."

The Master said, "The superior man, in the world, does not set his mind either for anything, or against anything; what is right he will follow."

The Master said, "He who exercises government by means of his virtue may be compared to the north polar star, which keeps its place and all the stars turn towards it."

The Master said, "In the government of a country, there are three things of essential importance: the nourishment of the people, the nourishment of the spirits, and the use of the people."

The Master said, "When a country is well governed, poverty and a mean condition are things to be ashamed of. When a country is ill governed, riches and honor are things to be ashamed of."

The Master said, "The man who is not trusted in a state, will never get the confidence of the people."

The Master said, "When a prince's personal conduct is correct, his government is effective without the issuing of orders. If his personal conduct is not correct, he may issue orders, but they will not be followed."

The Master said, "The governments of Lu and Wei are brothers."

The Master said of Jing, Duke of Wei, that he understood the regulating of the household.

### 原文
阳货欲见孔子，孔子不见，归孔子豚。孔子时其亡也，而往拜之。遇诸涂。谓孔子曰：“来！予与尔言。”曰：“怀其宝而迷其邦，可谓仁乎？”曰：“不可。”“好从事而亟失时，可谓知乎？”曰：“不可。”“日月逝矣，岁不我与。”孔子曰：“诺，吾将仕矣。”
子曰：“性相近也，习相远也。”
子曰：“唯上知与下愚不移。”
子之燕居，申申如也，夭夭如也。
子曰：“君子义以为上，君子有勇而无义为乱，小人有勇而无义为盗。”
子曰：“唯仁者能好人，能恶人。”
子曰：“苟志于仁矣，无恶也。”
子曰：“富与贵，是人之所欲也；不以其道得之，不处也。贫与贱，是人之所恶也；不以其道得之，不去也。君子去仁，恶乎成名？君子无终食之间违仁，造次必于是，颠沛必于是。”
子曰：“我未见好仁者，恶不仁者。好仁者，无以尚之；恶不仁者，其为仁矣，不使不仁者加乎其身。有能一日用其力于仁矣乎？我未见力不足者。盖有之矣，我未之见也。”
子曰：“人之过也，各于其党。观过，斯知仁矣。”
子曰：“朝闻道，夕死可矣。”
子曰：“士志于道，而耻恶衣恶食者，未足与议也。”
子曰：“君子之于天下也，无适也，无莫也，义之与比。”
子曰：“君子怀德，小人怀土；君子怀刑，小人怀惠。”
子曰：“放于利而行，多怨。”
子曰：“能以礼让为国乎，何有？不能以礼让为国，如礼何？”
子曰：“不患无位，患所以立；不患莫己知，求为可知也。”
子曰：“参乎！吾道一以贯之。”曾子曰：“唯。”子出，门人问曰：“何谓也？”曾子曰：“夫子之道，忠恕而已矣。”
子曰：“君子喻于义，小人喻于利。”
子曰：“见贤思齐焉，见不贤而内自省也。”
子曰：“事父母几谏，见志不从，又敬不违，劳而不怨。”
子曰：“父母在，不远游，游必有方。”
子曰：“三年无改于父之道，可谓孝矣。”
子曰：“父母之年，不可不知也。一则以喜，一则以惧。”
子曰：“古者言之不出，耻躬之不逮也。”
子曰：“以约失之者鲜矣。”
子曰：“君子欲讷于言而敏于行。”
子曰：“德不孤，必有邻。”
子游曰：“事君数，斯辱矣；朋友数，斯疏矣。”
